## Consensus algorithm

Fizen uses a delegated Bonded Proof-of-Stake (dBPoS) variant layered on top of a Tendermint-style Byzantine Fault Tolerant (BFT) consensus algorithm. The combination gives you:

- **Deterministic, single-slot finality** — a block is irreversible the moment it collects a two-thirds supermajority of pre-commits from the active validator set.
- **Liveness under adversarial conditions** — the protocol makes progress as long as more than two-thirds of bonded voting power is online and honest.
- **Predictable block times** — the slot duration is fixed at the protocol level; block production is not race-based the way proof-of-work is.

<Info>
  The BFT safety guarantee is precise: if fewer than one-third of total bonded
  voting power is Byzantine (dishonest or compromised), the protocol cannot
  commit two conflicting blocks at the same height. This is a hard mathematical
  guarantee, not a probabilistic one.
</Info>

### Why delegated stake

Pure BFT requires every participant to directly vote, which limits scalability. Delegated stake solves this by letting token holders assign their voting power to a validator they trust. The validator votes on their behalf. Delegation is non-custodial — delegators retain ownership of their tokens and can redelegate or undelegate at any time, subject to an unbonding period.

## How validators are selected and rotated

### Joining the active set

To become an active validator, an operator bonds a minimum stake of FZN tokens to the staking module. Token holders can delegate additional stake to any candidate validator. The active validator set consists of the top _N_ validators by total bonded stake, where _N_ is a governance parameter (initially set to 150).

Candidates outside the top _N_ are in the inactive set. They are eligible to move into the active set if a current validator is slashed, jailed, or if their own bonded stake surpasses that of the lowest-ranked active validator.

### Voting power

Each validator's voting power is proportional to their total bonded stake (own bond plus delegations). Voting power is recalculated at the end of every epoch (a fixed number of blocks). A single validator cannot exceed a governance-enforced cap on voting power to prevent excessive concentration.

<Warning>
  Validators approaching the voting power cap are a centralization signal.
  Delegators are encouraged to distribute stake across a diverse set of
  validators to preserve network decentralization.
</Warning>

### Rotation

The active validator set does not change every block. Set changes happen at epoch boundaries, when:

- A new candidate's bonded stake exceeds the lowest active validator's stake.
- An active validator is jailed due to downtime or slashing.
- An active validator voluntarily unbonds and exits.

This epoch-based rotation gives the networking layer time to stabilize and avoids disruptive mid-block validator churn.

## Block production and finality rules

Each slot is assigned a single block proposer, selected via a weighted round-robin algorithm proportional to voting power. The proposer rotates every slot, ensuring all validators get a turn proportional to their stake.

### Round structure

A single consensus round proceeds as follows:

<Steps>
  <Step title="Propose">
    The elected proposer assembles a block from the mempool, signs it, and
    broadcasts it to all validators.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Pre-vote">
    Each validator independently validates the proposed block. If valid, it
    broadcasts a signed pre-vote for that block. If the proposer is offline or
    the block is invalid, validators pre-vote for `nil`. Pre-votes are gossiped
    among all validators.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Pre-commit">
    Once a validator observes pre-votes from more than two-thirds of voting
    power for the same block (a "polka"), it broadcasts a signed pre-commit for
    that block. If no polka is reached, it pre-commits `nil`.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Commit">
    Once more than two-thirds of voting power has pre-committed the same block,
    the block is committed. Validators broadcast the full commit (the collection
    of pre-commit signatures) so any full node can independently verify
    finality.
  </Step>
</Steps>

### Timeouts and round advancement

If a round does not complete within its timeout window (due to a slow or absent proposer, network partition, etc.), validators advance to the next round with a new proposer. Round timeouts increase exponentially to allow for transient network delays without spinning too fast. The protocol guarantees liveness: as long as the honest supermajority is eventually connected, a block will eventually be committed.

### Finality

A committed block is final by protocol rule. No validator can sign a conflicting pre-commit for a different block at the same height without producing cryptographic evidence of equivocation — which is a slashable offense. You can safely treat any committed transaction as settled without waiting for additional confirmations.

## Fork resolution and chain reorganization handling

Because Fizen achieves single-slot finality, the canonical fork resolution question that plagues longest-chain protocols does not apply under normal operation. There is only one valid chain: the one with committed blocks.

### The only valid fork scenario

A fork at a committed height would require a Byzantine supermajority — more than one-third of bonded voting power colluding to sign conflicting commits. This is the protocol's primary security assumption. If this assumption breaks:

1. Both forks are invalid from the protocol's perspective.
2. The on-chain slashing evidence module can process the conflicting commit signatures and slash the offending validators.
3. The community coordinates an out-of-band social consensus process to determine which fork is canonical, similar to hard-fork governance on any BFT chain.

<Note>
  In practice, the economic cost of executing a one-third attack is prohibitive.
  Slashing destroys the attacker's entire bonded stake, and the attack is
  publicly attributable — the signed equivocation evidence is on-chain for
  anyone to see.
</Note>

### Network partitions

During a network partition, neither partition can commit new blocks unless it contains more than two-thirds of voting power. The larger partition resumes block production once connectivity is restored. The smaller partition stalls but does not produce a conflicting chain, preserving safety at the cost of temporary liveness.

## Economic incentives for honest participation

Fizen's security model relies on aligning validator self-interest with honest behavior through a combination of rewards and penalties.

### Block rewards

Validators receive newly minted FZN tokens for each block they help commit. Rewards are distributed in proportion to voting power. A small community tax is withheld from each block reward and directed to the on-chain treasury, which funds protocol development via governance.

### Transaction fees

All transaction fees collected in a block flow to the proposer and the participating validators, split according to a protocol-defined formula. The proposer receives a slightly larger share as an incentive to keep blocks full and well-formed.

### Delegator commissions

Validators charge a commission rate on the rewards earned by their delegators. Commission rates are set by the validator and are visible on-chain. Delegators choose validators partly on commission rate and partly on track record (uptime, governance participation).

### Penalties

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Downtime slashing">
    Validators that miss more than a threshold percentage of votes within a
    sliding window are jailed and subject to a small slash of their bonded
    stake. Jailed validators cannot propose or vote until they submit an
    unjailing transaction, which requires waiting out a minimum jail duration.

    ```text
    Downtime threshold : 5% of votes missed in a 10,000-block window
    Slash amount       : 0.01% of bonded stake
    Jail duration      : 10 minutes minimum
    ```
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Double-sign slashing">
    Validators that sign two conflicting blocks at the same height (equivocation)
    are permanently jailed and subject to a severe slash of their bonded stake.
    The evidence can be submitted by any full node, and the submitter receives a
    small finder's fee from the slashed amount.

    ```text
    Slash amount : 5% of bonded stake
    Jailing      : Permanent (requires governance to unjail)
    Evidence TTL : Valid for up to 21 days after the offense
    ```
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

<Tip>
  As a delegator, your bonded stake is also subject to slashing if your chosen
  validator misbehaves. Choose validators with a strong uptime record and
  transparent operations to minimize this risk.
</Tip>
